$p$-Laplacian: a conditional analysis}
\author{Hikmatullo Ismatov}

\email{hikmatullo.ismatov@kaust.edu.sa}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\begin{abstract}
We study the two-phase nonlocal dead-core problem
$\fpl{u}=u_+^{\gamma}-u_-^{\gamma}$ governed by the fractional $p$-Laplacian,
with $0<\gamma<p-1$, $s\in(0,1)$ and $p\ge2$. The natural scaling predicts the
improved growth $|u(x)|\le C|x-x_0|^{\beta}$, $\beta=\frac{sp}{p-1-\gamma}$, at
flat branching points. We carry out an honest analysis of the blow-up
strategy. The scaling identity and the disappearance of the absorption term
along the amplified blow-up are unconditional. We show that direct compactness
of the normalized blow-ups is \emph{obstructed} by a tail blow-up, and that the
first increments of an entire $(s,p)$-harmonic function solve a \emph{linear}
nonlocal equation; the existing regularity theory (Brasco--Lindgren--Schikorra,
Giovagnoli--Jesus--Silvestre) provides \emph{local} H\"older estimates for this
linearized equation but \emph{not} the global, scale-uniform Liouville theorem
that the argument needs. Accordingly we state the growth estimate
\emph{conditionally}, isolating the missing linearized Liouville input, the
compactness/tail control, and the inhomogeneous $C^{1}$-regularity required to
make sense of flatness. We also record the admissible parameter range for the
increment route and note that, for $p\neq2$, the usable cap is
$\beta<1+\asp$, not $\beta<2$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%======================================================================
We study the two-phase nonlocal problem
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main}
  \fpl{u}=u_+^{\gamma}-u_-^{\gamma}\qquad\text{in }B_1\subset\R^n,
\end{equation}
where $\fpl{}$ is the fractional $p$-Laplacian (Section~\ref{sec:prelim}),
$0<\gamma<p-1$, $s\in(0,1)$ and $p\ge2$. Such equations model diffusion in
the presence of a strong absorption: when the reaction order $\gamma$ is
sublinear, the solution may vanish on a whole interior region, the
\emph{dead core}, on whose boundary $u$ touches zero together with its
gradient. The non-Lipschitz character of $u\mapsto u^{\gamma}$ at the origin
is what permits dead cores to form.

Two features distinguish \eqref{eq:main} from the classical theory. It is
\emph{nonlocal}: $\fpl{u}(x)$ depends on $u$ over all of $\R^n$, so the
maximum-principle techniques of the local one-phase theory are unavailable.
And it is \emph{two-phase}: $u$ is not sign-constrained, so the points where
it touches zero need not be extrema. The one-phase signed problem is, in
addition, obstructed nonlocally by the strong minimum principle, which makes
the two-phase formulation \eqref{eq:main} the natural setting.

The exponent dictated by the scaling of \eqref{eq:main}
(Proposition~\ref{prop:scaling}) is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta}
  \beta:=\frac{sp}{\,p-1-\gamma\,},
\end{equation}
the analogue of $\frac{2s}{1-\gamma}$ in the fractional Laplacian case
$p=2$ of dos Prazeres, Teymurazyan and Urbano~\cite{PTU}.

\subsection{Strategy and the present obstruction}
The general regularity theory of the fractional $p$-Laplacian provides a
baseline regularity scale for solutions of \eqref{eq:main}: by Biswas and
Topp~\cite{BT} bounded solutions are $C^{0,\gamma_\circ}$ with
$\gamma_\circ=\min\{1,\frac{sp}{p-1}\}$, hence locally Lipschitz when
$\frac{sp}{p-1}>1$. The free-boundary scaling \eqref{eq:beta} predicts a
\emph{better} exponent $\beta$ at branching points, and the natural route to it
is a blow-up argument: rescale $u$ around the branching point, show the
absorption term vanishes in the limit, and classify the limit by a
Liouville-type theorem. This is the scheme of~\cite{PTU} for $p=2$ and of
Correa--dos Prazeres~\cite{CP} for the local $p$-Laplacian.

Unlike those settings, the nonlinear \emph{nonlocal} operator creates two
serious difficulties that this paper isolates rather than hides.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \emph{Compactness/tails.} Interior H\"older estimates for $\fpl{}$ carry
nonlocal tail terms. We show (Proposition~\ref{prop:tailobstruction}) that the
naturally normalized blow-up sequence has a \emph{divergent} tail, so its
compactness does \emph{not} follow from local $L^\infty$ bounds alone.
\item \emph{Nonlinearity in the Liouville step.} For $p\neq2$ differences of
solutions are not solutions, so the linear-nonlocal Liouville theorem
of~\cite{PTU} cannot be copied. We observe that first increments solve a
\emph{linear} nonlocal equation (Proposition~\ref{prop:linincr}); the
literature~\cite{BLS,GJS} gives \emph{local} H\"older regularity for that
equation, but the \emph{global, scale-uniform} Liouville theorem the argument
needs is not available and is isolated here as a hypothesis~$(\star)$.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently we state the growth estimate as a \emph{conditional} theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}), with all missing analytic inputs collected in
Section~\ref{sec:open}.

\subsection{What ``improved regularity'' should mean}
For $p=2$ the comparison is clean: once $u$ is Lipschitz the right-hand side
of $-(-\Delta)^s u=u_+^\gamma-u_-^\gamma$ is $C^\gamma$, Schauder theory gives
the baseline $C^{2s+\gamma}$, and $\frac{2s}{1-\gamma}$ exceeds it for
$s>\tfrac12$. For the fractional $p$-Laplacian the operator is nonlinear and
degenerate, and a Schauder statement $f\in C^\gamma\Rightarrow u\in
C^{sp+\gamma}$ for $\fpl{u}=f$ is \emph{not} among the inputs currently
available (see Remark~\ref{rem:baseline}). We therefore keep two exponents
separate. The scaling exponent $\beta$ is unconditional. A \emph{formal}
Schauder baseline $sp+\gamma$, treating the operator as of order $sp$, makes
the estimate a genuine improvement precisely when
\begin{equation}\label{eq:improve}
  \frac{sp}{p-1-\gamma}>sp+\gamma
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \gamma^2+(sp-(p-1))\gamma+sp(2-p)>0 .
\end{equation}
For $p=2$ this reads $\gamma^2+(2s-1)\gamma>0$, valid for all $\gamma>0$ when
$s>\tfrac12$; for $p>2$ it becomes $\gamma>\gamma_S$ with
$\gamma_S=\frac{(p-1)-sp+\sqrt{(sp-(p-1))^2+4sp(p-2)}}{2}$.
The third relevant exponent is $1+\asp$, where $\asp=\asp(n)\in(0,1)$ is the
H\"older exponent furnished by the regularity theory for the linearized
increment equation (Section~\ref{sec:liouville}). It is \emph{not} a Schauder
baseline but the threshold of the Liouville step, which requires
$\beta<1+\asp$.

\subsection{The admissible parameter range for the increment route}
The increment route (Section~\ref{sec:liouville}) carries, besides $0<\gamma<p-1$,
two constraints: a tail condition and the Liouville cap. The tail condition is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tailcond}
  \beta(p-1)<sp+1
  \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
  \boxed{\ \gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}\ }
\end{equation}
(see Remark~\ref{rem:tailcond}), and the Liouville cap is $\beta<1+\asp$. We
work in the range of~\cite{GJS},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gjs-range}
  p\ge2,\qquad p<\frac{2}{1-s}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad s>1-\frac2p,
\end{equation}
which is exactly the range in which the linearized operator is elliptic of
positive order $sp-p+2$ (Remark~\ref{rem:order}). For a cleaner first theorem
we may further restrict to the Lipschitz regime
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lip-range}
  sp>p-1\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad s>1-\frac1p,
\end{equation}
in which $\beta>1$ is automatic and the flat branching condition is
meaningful. The clean hypothesis block is then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hyp-block}
  p\ge2,\qquad s>1-\frac1p,\qquad p<\frac{2}{1-s},\qquad
  0<\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp},\qquad \frac{sp}{p-1-\gamma}<1+\asp .
\end{equation}
A direct computation (Remark~\ref{rem:nonempty}) shows that, with the cap
written either as $\beta<2$ or as $\beta<1+\asp$ for any $\asp>0$, the
resulting $\gamma$-range is nonempty \emph{if and only if} $p<\frac2{1-s}$; and
at $p=2$ the tail condition \eqref{eq:tailcond} reduces to PTU's
$\gamma<\frac1{1+2s}$.

\subsection{Main result}
The robust part of the argument is unconditional
(Propositions~\ref{prop:scaling}, \ref{prop:homog}, \ref{prop:linincr}); the
growth estimate is conditional.

\begin{theorem}[Conditional growth estimate; informal]\label{thm:main-intro}
Assume \eqref{eq:hyp-block}, the compactness/admissibility
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}, and the linearized Liouville
input~$(\star)$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:liouville-incr}. Let $u\in L^\infty(\R^n)$
solve \eqref{eq:main} with $0$ a flat branching point
(Definition~\ref{def:branch}). Then $|u(x)|\le C|x|^{\beta}$ for $x\in B_{1/2}$,
with $C=C(n,s,p,\gamma)$.
\end{theorem}

Letting $s\nearrow1$ \emph{formally} yields the analogous estimate for the
local two-phase problem $\Delta_p u=u_+^\gamma-u_-^\gamma$ with exponent
$\frac{p}{p-1-\gamma}$ (Section~\ref{sec:consequences}), which does not appear
to be recorded in the literature; this limit is itself conditional, as the
constants of~\cite{GJS} are not uniform as $s\to1$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Organization.} Section~\ref{sec:prelim} fixes notation, the
notion of solution, the scaling and homogeneity identities, the increment

Section~\ref{sec:liouville} treats the Liouville step, both the conditional

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:prelim}
%======================================================================

\subsection{Notation and the operator}
$B_r(x_0)$ is the open ball of radius $r$ at $x_0$, $B_r:=B_r(0)$, and
$u_\pm:=\max\{\pm u,0\}$. With $J_p(t):=\Jp{t}$,
\[
  \fpl{u}(x):=\mathrm{P.V.}\!\int_{\R^n}
    \frac{J_p\big(u(x)-u(y)\big)}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,dy,
\]
the normalizing constant being omitted. The nonlocal tail is
\[
  \Tail(u;x_0,R):=\Big(R^{sp}\!\int_{\R^n\setminus B_R(x_0)}
    \frac{|u(y)|^{p-1}}{|y-x_0|^{n+sp}}\,dy\Big)^{\!\frac{1}{p-1}}.
\]
Solutions of \eqref{eq:main} are taken in the weak sense, with
$u\in W^{s,p}_{loc}\cap L^\infty(\R^n)$ and finite tail; weak and viscosity
solutions coincide~\cite{KKL}.

\subsection{Scaling and homogeneity}
\begin{proposition}[Scaling]\label{prop:scaling}
For $A>0$, $r>0$ and $v(x):=u(rx)/A$,
\[
  \fpl{v}(x)=A^{1-p}\,r^{\,sp}\,(\fpl{u})(rx).
\]
In particular, for $u_r(x):=u(rx)/r^{\beta}$ (i.e.\ $A=r^{\beta}$),
$\fpl{u_r}(x)=r^{\,sp-\beta(p-1)}(\fpl{u})(rx)$, and $u_r$ solves
\eqref{eq:main} in $B_{1/r}$ if and only if $sp-\beta(p-1)+\beta\gamma=0$, i.e.\
$\beta=\frac{sp}{p-1-\gamma}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since $v(x)-v(y)=A^{-1}(u(rx)-u(ry))$ and $J_p$ is $(p-1)$-homogeneous,
$\fpl{v}(x)=A^{1-p}\mathrm{P.V.}\!\int J_p(u(rx)-u(ry))|x-y|^{-n-sp}\,dy$; the
substitution $z=ry$ contributes $r^{sp}$, giving the first identity. Taking
$A=r^{\beta}$ and using $(u_r)_\pm=r^{-\beta}u_\pm(r\cdot)$, the right-hand side
of \eqref{eq:main} scales by $r^{-\beta\gamma}$; balancing the powers of $r$
gives \eqref{eq:beta}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Amplified blow-up: vanishing right-hand side]\label{prop:homog}
Let $u_k$ solve \eqref{eq:main} with $\|u_k\|_{L^\infty}\le M$, let $r_k\to0$,
$\theta_k\to\infty$, and
\[
  \theta_k=\frac{\sup_{B_{r_k}}|u_k|}{r_k^{\beta}},\qquad
  v_k(x):=\frac{u_k(r_kx)}{\theta_k r_k^{\beta}}.
\]
Then
\[
  \fpl{v_k}=\theta_k^{\,\gamma-(p-1)}
   \big[(v_k)_+^{\gamma}-(v_k)_-^{\gamma}\big]\quad\text{in }B_{1/r_k},
\]
and since $\gamma<p-1$ the coefficient $\theta_k^{\gamma-(p-1)}\to0$, so the
absorption term vanishes in the limit. We do \emph{not} conclude that a limit
$v_0$ is $(s,p)$-harmonic in the standard sense; see
Remark~\ref{rem:renorm}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
With $A=\theta_k r_k^\beta$, Proposition~\ref{prop:scaling} gives
$\fpl{v_k}=(\theta_k r_k^\beta)^{1-p}r_k^{sp}(\fpl{u_k})(r_k\cdot)$. Inserting
the equation and $(v_k)_\pm=(\theta_kr_k^\beta)^{-1}(u_k)_\pm(r_k\cdot)$
produces the factor $(\theta_kr_k^\beta)^{\gamma-(p-1)}r_k^{sp}
=\theta_k^{\gamma-(p-1)}$, since $\beta(p-1-\gamma)=sp$. The passage to the
limit requires a stability statement for $\fpl{}$ under local uniform
convergence \emph{with tail control}; the latter is not automatic
(Proposition~\ref{prop:tailobstruction}) and is part of
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The limiting equation must be renormalized]\label{rem:renorm}
The expected growth $|v_0(x)|\lesssim|x|^{\beta}$ has $\beta(p-1)>sp$, so $v_0$
need not lie in the tail space $L^{p-1}_{sp}(\R^n)$ on which $\fpl{v_0}$ is
defined; thus ``$\fpl{v_0}=0$'' is \emph{not} literally well-posed. This
already occurs for $p=2$ in~\cite{PTU}, where the limit is handled only through
its increments: $(-\Delta)^s\big(v_0(\cdot+h)-v_0\big)=0$, which is well defined
because increments grow one order slower. We adopt the same convention: the
limiting statement is the \emph{increment} equation $L_{K}w_0^h=0$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:linincr}), meaningful under the tail
condition~\eqref{eq:tailcond}. A rigorous framework for this is the notion of
Dipierro--Savin--Valdinoci~\cite{DSV} of solutions of polynomial growth, in
which the equation is read \emph{modulo polynomials}: a constant (or affine)
right-hand side is identified with $0$. For $p=2$ this makes precise the
second-difference cancellation of~\cite{PTU}. For $p\neq2$, however, the
increment kernel is variable, degenerate and non-translation-invariant, and
\cite{DSV} (stated for the fractional Laplacian) does not cover it; the
corresponding modulo-polynomial theory for $L_{K_h}$ is an open analytic input
(Section~\ref{sec:open}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Increments solve a linear equation}
\begin{proposition}[Linearization along increments]\label{prop:linincr}
For $v\in C^1_{loc}\cap L^\infty_{loc}$ and $h\in\R^n$, set $w^h:=v(\cdot+h)-v$.
Then, in the weak sense, for every $\varphi\in C^\infty_c(\R^n)$,
\[
  \iint_{\R^n\times\R^n}\big(w^h(x)-w^h(y)\big)\big(\varphi(x)-\varphi(y)\big)
   \frac{K_h(x,y)}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,dx\,dy
   = \langle\fpl{[v(\cdot+h)]}-\fpl{v},\varphi\rangle,
\]
with the \emph{symmetric, nonnegative} kernel
\[
  K_h(x,y)=(p-1)\int_0^1\big|(1-t)(v(x)-v(y))+t\,(v(x+h)-v(y+h))\big|^{p-2}dt .
\]
In particular, if $v$ is $(s,p)$-harmonic then $w^h$ is a weak solution of the
\emph{linear} nonlocal equation $L_{K_h}w^h=0$ --- though not of the
homogeneous fractional $p$-Laplacian.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since $\fpl{}$ is translation invariant, $V:=v(\cdot+h)$ is $(s,p)$-harmonic
whenever $v$ is. Subtracting the weak formulations of $V$ and $v$ tested
against $\varphi$, and using the identity (valid for $p\ge2$, where $J_p\in
C^1$)
\[
  J_p(b)-J_p(a)=(p-1)\Big(\int_0^1|a+t(b-a)|^{p-2}dt\Big)(b-a),\quad
  a=v(x)-v(y),\ b=V(x)-V(y),
\]
with $b-a=w^h(x)-w^h(y)$, yields the displayed bilinear identity.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Weak increment identity for the blow-up sequence]
\label{cor:incr-weak}
Let $v_k$ solve $\fpl{v_k}=\theta_k^{\gamma-(p-1)}\Phi(v_k)$ weakly in
$B_{\rho_k}$, $\rho_k:=1/r_k$, with $\Phi(t)=t_+^{\gamma}-t_-^{\gamma}$, and fix
$h\in\R^n$, $|h|<\rho_k/4$. Set $W_k^h:=v_k(\cdot+h)-v_k$ and let
\[
  \Omega_k^h:=\{x\in\R^n:\ x\in B_{\rho_k}\text{ and }x+h\in B_{\rho_k}\}
  \supset B_{\rho_k/2}
\]
be the common admissible domain. Then for every
$\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\Omega_k^h)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weak-incr-full}
  \frac12\iint_{\R^n\times\R^n}
   a_k^h(x,y)\,
   \frac{\big(W_k^h(x)-W_k^h(y)\big)\big(\varphi(x)-\varphi(y)\big)}
        {|x-y|^{n+sp}}\;dx\,dy
  \;=\;\int_{\R^n} f_k^h\,\varphi\,dx,
\end{equation}
with
\[
  a_k^h(x,y)=(p-1)\int_0^1\big|B_k(x,y)+t\big(A_k(x,y)-B_k(x,y)\big)\big|^{p-2}dt,
  \qquad
  \begin{aligned}
   A_k(x,y)&=v_k(x+h)-v_k(y+h),\\
   B_k(x,y)&=v_k(x)-v_k(y),
  \end{aligned}
\]
and
\[
  f_k^h:=\theta_k^{\gamma-(p-1)}\big[\Phi\big(v_k(\cdot+h)\big)-\Phi(v_k)\big].
\]
Moreover $f_k^h\to0$ in $L^\infty_{loc}$: on any fixed ball $B_R$ where
$\sup_k\|v_k\|_{L^\infty(B_{R+|h|})}\le C_R$,
\[
  \|f_k^h\|_{L^\infty(B_R)}
  \le 2\,\theta_k^{\gamma-(p-1)}\,C_R^{\gamma}\ \longrightarrow\ 0
  \qquad(k\to\infty),
\]
since $\gamma<p-1$ and $\theta_k\to\infty$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Test the weak formulation of the equation for $v_k(\cdot+h)$ (valid in
$B_{\rho_k}-h\supset\Omega_k^h$, by translating the formulation for $v_k$)
and that for $v_k$ against the same $\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\Omega_k^h)$, and
subtract. On the left, apply the identity of
Proposition~\ref{prop:linincr} pointwise in $(x,y)$ (legitimate for $p\ge2$,
where $J_p\in C^1(\R)$, by the fundamental theorem of calculus); the symmetric
$\frac12\iint$ normalization matches the convention in
\eqref{eq:weak-incr-full}. Every integral converges absolutely: near the
diagonal, $a_k^h(x,y)\le(p-1)(|A_k|+|B_k|)^{p-2}\lesssim|x-y|^{p-2}$ by the
local Lipschitz bound for $v_k$, so the integrand is
$O(|x-y|^{p-n-sp})$ with $p>sp$; in the far field, the tail hypotheses of
\S\ref{ssec:truncdiff} apply. On the right, $|\Phi(a)-\Phi(b)|\le
2\max(|a|,|b|)^{\gamma}$ for $0<\gamma\le1$ (and
$\le\gamma\max(|a|,|b|)^{\gamma-1}|a-b|$ for $\gamma\ge1$), giving the stated
$L^\infty_{loc}$ bound and the convergence $f_k^h\to0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Degeneracy and order of the linearized kernel]\label{rem:order}
When $v$ is differentiable, $K_h(x,y)\sim(p-1)|Dv(x)|^{p-2}|x-y|^{p-2}$ near
the diagonal, so $K_h(x,y)|x-y|^{-n-sp}$ is of homogeneity $-n-(sp-(p-2))$:
the linearized operator $L_{K_h}$ has order $sp-p+2$, positive precisely when
$s>1-\frac2p$, i.e.\ in the range \eqref{eq:gjs-range}. For $p>2$ the weight
$|Dv|^{p-2}$ \emph{vanishes where $Dv=0$} and grows in the far field; thus
$L_{K_h}$ is \emph{degenerate} at critical points of $v$ and is not uniformly
elliptic globally. This is a serious issue here, because the blow-up limit is
flat at the origin in the sup sense ($\sup_{B_r}|v_0|=o(r)$ along the
normalization; the pointwise statement $Dv_0(0)=0$ would require the
$C^1_{loc}$-compactness of Assumption~\ref{ass:compact} and is not asserted
unconditionally), exactly where the kernel degenerates.

\emph{Warning (three exponents, not one).} The coefficient $K_h$ scales
differently in three regimes and these must not be conflated:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \emph{near-diagonal:} $K_h\sim|Dv(x)|^{p-2}|x-y|^{p-2}$ --- governs the
  \emph{order} $sp-p+2$ of $L_{K_h}$;
\item \emph{near a flat point:} the weight $|Dv(x)|^{p-2}\sim
  |x|^{(\beta-1)(p-2)}$ --- governs the \emph{degeneracy at the origin}
  (route~(W) of \S\ref{sec:open});
\item \emph{far field:} $K_h(x,y)\sim|v(y)|^{p-2}\sim|y|^{\beta(p-2)}$ for
  $x$ in a fixed ball and $|y|\to\infty$ --- the coefficient sees the
  \emph{function values}, not the increments; this exponent, and not
  $(\beta-1)(p-2)$, is the one entering the tail condition
  \eqref{eq:tailcond}.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Regularity inputs}
We record the two regularity facts we use. Both are \emph{local}.

\begin{lemma}[Inhomogeneous H\"older/Lipschitz baseline~\cite{BT}]\label{lem:bt}
Let $p>1$, $s\in(0,1)$ and let $u$ be a bounded weak solution of $\fpl{u}=f$
in $B_2$ with $f\in L^\infty(B_2)$ and $\frac{sp}{p-1}\neq1$. Then
$u\in C^{0,\gamma_\circ}_{loc}(B_2)$ with
$\gamma_\circ=\min\{1,\frac{sp}{p-1}\}$; in particular $u$ is locally Lipschitz
when $\frac{sp}{p-1}>1$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Homogeneous $C^{1,\al_0}$ estimate and local linearized regularity~\cite{GJS}]\label{lem:gjs}
Let $2\le p<\frac{2}{1-s}$. If $v$ is $(s,p)$-harmonic in $B_2$ with
$\Tail(v;0,2)<\infty$, then $v\in C^{1,\al_0}(B_1)$ for some
$\al_0=\al_0(n,s,p)\in(0,1)$, with
$\|v\|_{C^{1,\al_0}(B_1)}\le C(\|v\|_{L^\infty(B_2)}+\Tail(v;0,2))$.
Moreover~\cite[\S7]{GJS} establishes a \emph{local} interior H\"older estimate
$[w]_{C^{\asp}(B_{1/2})}\le C(\|w\|_{L^\infty(\R^n)}+\|f\|_{L^\infty(B_1)})$ for
weak solutions of the linear equation $L_K w=f$ with a measurable kernel $K$
satisfying symmetry, a scaled upper bound, and a coercivity (ellipticity)
hypothesis --- the latter requiring the kernel to be \emph{nondegenerate}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}[The Schauder baseline; flatness is conditional]\label{rem:baseline}
Lemma~\ref{lem:bt} is the best presently available regularity for solutions of
\eqref{eq:main}: $C^{0,\gamma_\circ}$, and Lipschitz exactly when
$\frac{sp}{p-1}>1$. A Schauder statement $f\in C^\gamma\Rightarrow u\in
C^{sp+\gamma}$, or even an \emph{inhomogeneous} $C^{1,\sigma}$ estimate for
$\fpl{u}=f\in L^\infty$, is \emph{not} available from~\cite{BT,GJS}.
Consequently two things are conditional and must be flagged. First, the
comparison \eqref{eq:improve} is against a \emph{formal} baseline. Second, the
condition $Du(x_0)=0$ in the definition of a flat branching point presupposes
$C^1$-regularity of $u$, which we do \emph{not} have for the inhomogeneous
equation; see Definition~\ref{def:branch}. Whether inhomogeneous fractional
$p$-Poisson regularity (e.g.\ B\"ogelein--Duzaar--Liao--Moring~\cite{BDLM})
upgrades Lemma~\ref{lem:bt} to a genuine interior $C^{1,\al}$ estimate, thereby
licensing $Du(x_0)=0$ and the $C^{1,\al'}$ blow-up device, is an open input
(Section~\ref{sec:open}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{The tail obstruction to compactness}
\begin{proposition}[Tail blow-up of the normalized sequence]\label{prop:tailobstruction}
Let $u_k$, $r_k$, $\theta_k$, $v_k$ be as in Proposition~\ref{prop:homog}, with
$\|u_k\|_{L^\infty(\R^n)}\le M$, and set $\varepsilon_k:=\sup_{B_{r_k}}|u_k|
=\theta_k r_k^{\beta}$, so that $v_k(y)=u_k(r_ky)/\varepsilon_k$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item $|v_k(y)|\le M/\varepsilon_k$ for all $y\in\R^n$;
\item if $0$ is a branching point and the $u_k$ are uniformly Lipschitz near
$0$ \textnormal{(Lemma~\ref{lem:bt}, $\frac{sp}{p-1}>1$)}, then
$\varepsilon_k\to0$, and the \emph{available} upper bounds
$|v_k(y)|\le\min\{C|y|^\beta,\,M/\varepsilon_k\}$ yield only
\[
  \Tail(v_k;0,R)^{\,p-1}\ \lesssim\ C(R,n,s,p)\,\varepsilon_k^{-\gamma},
\]
a bound that diverges as $k\to\infty$; in particular these bounds furnish
\emph{no} uniform-in-$k$ tail control.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
(1) is immediate from $|u_k|\le M$. For (2): at a branching point with
$u_k(0)=0$ and uniform Lipschitz bound $L$, $\varepsilon_k\le Lr_k\to0$. Split
the tail of $v_k$ at the crossover $\rho_k=(M/C\varepsilon_k)^{1/\beta}$ of the
two bounds $|v_k|\le C|y|^\beta$ and $|v_k|\le M/\varepsilon_k$; using
$\beta(p-1)-sp=\frac{\gamma sp}{p-1-\gamma}>0$, both regions are bounded by the
order $C(R)\varepsilon_k^{-\gamma}$ (the exponent identity $\delta/\beta=\gamma$,
$\delta=\beta(p-1)-sp$), which diverges as $\varepsilon_k\to0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:tail-upper}
The estimate of Proposition~\ref{prop:tailobstruction}(2) is an
\emph{upper}-bound obstruction: the best available bounds permit tail growth of
order $\varepsilon_k^{-\gamma}$, which is why standard H\"older compactness
cannot be obtained from local $L^\infty$-bounds. We do \emph{not} assert a
matching lower bound $\Tail(v_k)\gtrsim\varepsilon_k^{-\gamma}$; that would
require a saturation (lower-mass-on-annuli) hypothesis on the $v_k$, which we
do not impose.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Truncation: oscillation control, but not constant control}
A natural attempt to bypass the tail obstruction is to truncate $v_k$ at an
intermediate scale and carry the far field as a datum. This controls the
\emph{oscillation} of the resulting error but not its (possibly divergent)
constant part. Fix $\eta\in C^1_c(\R^n)$ with $0\le\eta\le1$, $\eta\equiv1$ on
$B_1$, $\eta\equiv0$ off $B_2$. For $R_k\to\infty$ set $\eta_k(y)=\eta(y/R_k)$
and $\tilde v_k:=\eta_k v_k$. For a fixed ball $B_R$ with $R\ll R_k$ one has
$\tilde v_k=v_k$ on $B_{R_k}$, so for $x\in B_R$ the singular part cancels and
\[
  E_k(x):=\fpl{\tilde v_k}(x)-\fpl{v_k}(x)
  =\int_{|y|>R_k}\frac{J_p(v_k(x)-\eta_k(y)v_k(y))-J_p(v_k(x)-v_k(y))}
   {|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,dy,
\]
absolutely convergent since $\tilde v_k$ is compactly supported.

\begin{lemma}[Truncation oscillation estimate]\label{lem:trunc}
Assume, for large $k$, $\sup_{B_R}|v_k|\le A$, $\sup_{B_R}|\nabla v_k|\le L_R$,
$|v_k(y)|\le A(1+|y|)^{\beta}$ for $|y|\le 2R_k$, and $|v_k(y)|\le B_k$ on
$\R^n$. Write $E_k=a_k+g_k$ with $a_k:=E_k(0)$, $g_k:=E_k-E_k(0)$. Then
\[
  \|\nabla g_k\|_{L^\infty(B_R)}\le C\Big[
   R_k^{\beta(p-2)-sp}+R_k^{\beta(p-1)-sp-1}
   +B_k^{p-2}R_k^{-sp}+B_k^{p-1}R_k^{-sp-1}\Big],
\]
$C=C(n,s,p,\eta,R,A,L_R)$. In the blow-up case $R_k\sim c/r_k$,
$B_k=M/(\theta_k r_k^{\beta})$, this yields $\|\nabla g_k\|_{L^\infty(B_R)}\to0$
provided
\[
  \beta(p-1)<sp+1
  \qquad\Big(\Longleftrightarrow\ \gamma<\tfrac{p-1}{1+sp}\Big),
\]
which, since $\beta>1$, also forces $\beta(p-2)<sp$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
With $N_k(x,y)=J_p(v_k(x)-\eta_k(y)v_k(y))-J_p(v_k(x)-v_k(y))$ one has
$E_k(x)=\int_{|y|>R_k}N_k(x,y)|x-y|^{-n-sp}dy$, and for $x\in B_R$,
$|y|>R_k\gg R$, $|x-y|\sim|y|$. Two contributions to $\nabla_x E_k$. (i)
\emph{Numerator}: since $J_p\in C^1$ with $|J_p'(t)|=(p-1)|t|^{p-2}$,
$|\nabla_xN_k(x,y)|\le C|\nabla v_k(x)|(1+|v_k(y)|)^{p-2}\le
CL_R(1+|v_k(y)|)^{p-2}$; splitting $\{R_k<|y|<2R_k\}$ (where $|v_k|\le A|y|^\beta$)
from $\{|y|>2R_k\}$ (where $|v_k|\le B_k$) gives the first and third terms. (ii)
\emph{Denominator}: $|\nabla_x|x-y|^{-n-sp}|\le C|y|^{-n-sp-1}$ and
$|N_k(x,y)|\le C(1+|v_k(y)|)^{p-1}$; the same splitting gives the second and
fourth terms. Substituting $R_k\sim c/r_k$, $B_k=M/(\theta_k r_k^\beta)$ and
using $\beta(p-1-\gamma)=sp$ gives the four terms
$r_k^{sp-\beta(p-2)},\,r_k^{sp+1-\beta(p-1)},\,
\theta_k^{-(p-2)}r_k^{sp-\beta(p-2)},\,\theta_k^{-(p-1)}r_k^{sp+1-\beta(p-1)}$,
all $\to0$ under $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Truncation controls oscillation, not the constant]\label{rem:trunc}
Under $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$, Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc} gives $E_k=a_k+o_{C^1(B_R)}(1)$:
truncation converts the far field into a \emph{nearly constant} forcing term.
It does \emph{not} bound $a_k$. Indeed
$|a_k|=|E_k(0)|\lesssim\int_{|y|>R_k}(1+|v_k(y)|)^{p-1}|y|^{-n-sp}dy\sim
R_k^{\beta(p-1)-sp}=r_k^{-\delta}$, with $\delta:=\beta(p-1)-sp>0$, which
\emph{diverges}; the $x$-oscillation gains exactly one power $|y|^{-1}\sim r_k$,
turning $r_k^{-\delta}$ into $r_k^{1-\delta}\to0$ (valid precisely when
$\delta<1$, i.e.\ $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$), but leaves the constant uncontrolled. For
$p=2$ the constant is removed by passing to (second) differences; for $p\neq2$
it cannot be removed from the nonlinear equation for $v_k$ itself, and no a
posteriori bound on $a_k$ follows from these hypotheses. Hence
\[
  \textbf{truncation alone does not close compactness for }v_k;
  \textbf{ it must be combined with differencing.}
\]
The hypothesis $\sup_{B_R}|\nabla v_k|\le L_R$ is itself part of the conditional
$C^1$ input (Remark~\ref{rem:baseline}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Truncation combined with differencing}\label{ssec:truncdiff}
The divergent constant $a_k$ of Remark~\ref{rem:trunc} can be removed by passing
to increments before truncating, \emph{provided} the constant either cancels
(as at $p=2$) or is controlled by a far-field increment estimate of the data.
We carry out the construction; the outcome is that the constant cancels for
$p=2$ but not for $p\neq2$, where its control is a genuinely new input.

Let $w_k^h:=v_k(\cdot+h)-v_k$. By Proposition~\ref{prop:linincr} and the
equation for $v_k$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:incr-eq-vk}
  L_{k,h}w_k^h=f_k^h\quad\text{in }B_{1/r_k},\qquad
  f_k^h:=\theta_k^{\gamma-(p-1)}\big[\Phi(v_k)(\cdot+h)-\Phi(v_k)\big]\to0,
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(t)=t_+^\gamma-t_-^\gamma$ and $L_{k,h}=L_{K_{k,h}}$ has the
increment kernel $K_{k,h}=(p-1)\int_0^1|(1-t)(v_k(x)-v_k(y))+t(v_k(x+h)-v_k(y+h))|^{p-2}dt$.
Truncate $\tilde w_k^h:=\eta(\cdot/R_k)w_k^h$, $R_k\sim c/r_k$; for $x\in B_R$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:trunc-incr}
  L_{k,h}\tilde w_k^h=f_k^h+a_k(h)+g_k^h,\qquad
  a_k(h):=F_k^h(0),\quad g_k^h:=F_k^h-F_k^h(0),
\end{equation}
where $F_k^h(x)=\int(1-\eta(y/R_k))w_k^h(y)\,K_{k,h}(x,y)|x-y|^{-n-sp}\,dy$.

\begin{lemma}[Increment truncation: oscillation control]\label{lem:trunc-incr}
Assume the local bounds $|v_k|+|\nabla v_k|\le C_R$ on $B_{R+|h|}$, the
far-field growth $|v_k(y)|\le C(1+|y|)^{\beta}$, hence
$|w_k^h(y)|\le C_h(1+|y|)^{\beta-1}$, and \textnormal{(}for the kernel-gradient
bound\textnormal{)} either $p\ge3$ or a uniform non-cancellation lower bound on
$K_{k,h}$. Then, for $x\in B_R$,
\[
  \|\nabla g_k^h\|_{L^\infty(B_R)}\le C_h\big(
   R_k^{\beta(p-2)-sp-1}+R_k^{\beta(p-1)-sp-2}\big)\ \longrightarrow\ 0
\]
under $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$ \textnormal{(}$\Leftrightarrow\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}$\textnormal{)}.
This decay is \emph{one power better} than the direct truncation
\textnormal{(}Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc}\textnormal{)}, since $w_k^h$ grows like
$|y|^{\beta-1}$ rather than $|y|^{\beta}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Near the far field $|x-y|\sim|y|$, $|K_{k,h}(x,y)|\le C_R(1+|y|)^{\beta(p-2)}$
and $|\nabla_xK_{k,h}(x,y)|\le C_R(1+|y|)^{\beta(p-3)}$ (the latter under the
$p\ge3$/non-cancellation hypothesis). Splitting $\nabla_xF_k^h$ into the
kernel-gradient term $I_1\lesssim\int_{R_k}^\infty\rho^{\beta-1+\beta(p-3)-sp-1}d\rho
=R_k^{\beta(p-2)-sp-1}$ and the denominator-gradient term
$I_2\lesssim\int_{R_k}^\infty\rho^{\beta-1+\beta(p-2)-sp-2}d\rho
=R_k^{\beta(p-1)-sp-2}$ gives the bound; both exponents are negative under
$\beta(p-1)<sp+1$.
\end{proof}

The \emph{constant} $a_k(h)$, however, behaves like the direct case. If the
increment growth $|w_k^h(y)|\le C_h(1+|y|)^{\beta-1}$ held \emph{globally} then
$|a_k(h)|\lesssim R_k^{\beta(p-1)-sp-1}=r_k^{1-\delta}\to0$
($\delta=\beta(p-1)-sp<1$). But for actual blow-ups only the global $L^\infty$
bound $|w_k^h|\le 2M/\varepsilon_k$ is available beyond $\sim1/r_k$, giving an
outer contribution $\sim M^{p-1}\theta_k^{-(p-1)}r_k^{\,sp-\beta(p-1)}
=M^{p-1}\theta_k^{-(p-1)}r_k^{-\delta}$, which need \emph{not} vanish.

\begin{proposition}[$p=2$: the constant cancels]\label{prop:p2cancel}
For $p=2$, $K_{k,h}\equiv c_{n,s}$ and $L_{k,h}=(-\Delta)^s$ is linear and
translation invariant, so $(-\Delta)^s\Delta_\ell=\Delta_\ell(-\Delta)^s$.
Applying a further difference to \eqref{eq:trunc-incr} gives
$(-\Delta)^s\Delta_\ell\tilde w_k^h=\Delta_\ell f_k^h+\Delta_\ell g_k^h$,
in which the constant $a_k(h)$ has cancelled ($\Delta_\ell a_k(h)=0$). Hence
truncation $+$ second differencing closes the constant-mode problem at $p=2$
\textnormal{(}the PTU mechanism\textnormal{)}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[$p\neq2$: the constant is not removed]\label{prop:pneq2}
For $p\neq2$ the cancellation of Proposition~\ref{prop:p2cancel} fails, for
three independent reasons.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \emph{No commutator identity.} Since $K_{k,h}$ depends on $v_k$,
$L_{k,h}\Delta_\ell\neq\Delta_\ell L_{k,h}$; the commutator
$\Delta_\ell(L_{k,h}\tilde w_k^h)-L_{k,h}\Delta_\ell\tilde w_k^h$ contains the
$x$-oscillation $K_{k,h}(x+\ell,y)|x+\ell-y|^{-n-sp}-K_{k,h}(x,y)|x-y|^{-n-sp}$,
which depends on $Dv_k$ and degenerates where $Dv_k=0$; it is not small under
the present hypotheses.
\item \emph{No exact cocycle for $a_k$.} While $w^{h+\ell}=w^h(\cdot+\ell)+w^\ell$,
the constants $a_k(h+\ell),a_k(h),a_k(\ell)$ carry the distinct kernels
$K_{k,h+\ell},K_{k,h},K_{k,\ell}$; the defect $a_k(h+\ell)-a_k(h)-a_k(\ell)$ is
a sum of kernel-difference integrals, small only under the same global
increment control that fails for blow-ups.
\item \emph{No a posteriori bound from local regularity.} Pointwise evaluation
of $L_{k,h}\tilde w_k^h(0)$ requires regularity above the operator order
$sp-p+2$, whereas \cite[\S7]{GJS} supplies only a H\"older estimate; and weak
testing $a_k(h)\int\varphi=\mathcal E_{K_{k,h}}(\tilde w_k^h,\varphi)-\int(f_k^h+g_k^h)\varphi$
needs a \emph{uniform energy bound} for $\tilde w_k^h$ in $K_{k,h}$, which is
itself another compactness/tail input. Hence no bound on $a_k(h)$ follows.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}[Conditional compactness for increments]\label{lem:truncdiff}
Assume $p\ge2$, $s>1-\frac1p$, $p<\frac2{1-s}$, $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$; the kernels
$K_{k,h}$ satisfy the GJS-type hypotheses on $B_{2R}$ \textnormal{(}symmetry,
scaled upper bound, coercivity of order $sp-p+2$\textnormal{)} with constants
independent of $k$; the local bounds of Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc-incr}; and the
\emph{constant-mode control}
\[
  \textnormal{(CM)}\qquad |a_k(h)|\le C_h\quad\text{(better: }a_k(h)\to0\text{)}.
\]
Then $\tilde w_k^h\to w_0^h$ in $C^{\asp}_{loc}$ along a subsequence, and the
limit increment solves $L_{K_0}w_0^h=c(h)$ with $c(h)=\lim a_k(h)$. If
$a_k(h)\to0$ the limit equation is homogeneous; if $a_k(h)\to a(h)\neq0$ it is
inhomogeneous with a constant right-hand side, which does \emph{not} suffice for
the Liouville step unless the constant can be removed.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}[The precise missing input]\label{rem:farfield}
By Propositions~\ref{prop:p2cancel}--\ref{prop:pneq2}, for $p\neq2$ the
constant-mode hypothesis \textnormal{(CM)} is \emph{not} a consequence of local
regularity, the cocycle, or differencing. It is equivalent to a
\emph{uniform far-field increment estimate of the data},
\[
  |v_k(y+h)-v_k(y)|\ \lesssim_h\ (1+|y|)^{\beta-1}\quad\text{globally in }y,
\]
i.e.\ $a_k(h)\to0$ --- which is precisely the information that local behavior
near the free boundary does not supply. This, not the size of the truncation
oscillation, is the exact remaining obstruction to compactness for $p\neq2$;
\[
  \textbf{truncation }+\textbf{ differencing closes }p=2\textbf{, but for }
  p\neq2\textbf{ it requires uniform far-field increment control.}
\]
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The constant as a degree-zero polynomial: the DSV viewpoint]\label{rem:dsv}
The constant $a_k(h)$ is a degree-$0$ polynomial. In the polynomial-growth
framework of Dipierro--Savin--Valdinoci~\cite{DSV}, where the equation is read
\emph{modulo polynomials}, such a constant is identified with $0$, so the
far-field constant is not part of the meaningful limit equation. At $p=2$ this
is exactly the second-difference mechanism (Proposition~\ref{prop:p2cancel}),
and \cite{DSV} makes it rigorous. For $p\neq2$ the same viewpoint indicates the
correct formulation --- $L_{K_h}w^h=0$ \emph{modulo constants} --- but
\cite{DSV}, and the measurable-kernel Schauder theory of~\cite{FRO,Serra}, are
established for \emph{uniformly elliptic} kernels, not for the variable,
degenerate kernel $K_h$. The needed modulo-polynomial stability/Liouville
theory for $L_{K_h}$ is therefore a genuine new input, not a citation
(Section~\ref{sec:open}). \emph{This reframes the obstruction: the constant is
not mysterious, it is a degree-zero polynomial obstruction; handled by the
\textnormal{DSV/PTU} framework at $p=2$, and requiring its degenerate-kernel
analogue for $p\neq2$.}
\end{remark}

Independently of the modulo-polynomial route, the constant can be made to
vanish under a regularity hypothesis on the \emph{exterior data} alone.

\begin{lemma}[Stability under exterior H\"older data]\label{lem:exterior}
Suppose the exterior data are uniformly H\"older,
$|u_k(x)-u_k(y)|\le C|x-y|^{\sigma}$ for $x,y\in\R^n\setminus B_1$, with
\[
  \sigma>\delta:=\beta(p-1)-sp\ \ (>0).
\]
Then the far-field constant of \eqref{eq:trunc-incr} satisfies
$|a_k(h)|\lesssim|h|^{\sigma}\,\theta_k^{-(p-1)}\,r_k^{\,\sigma-\delta}\to0$. In
particular, since $\delta<1$ under the tail condition~\eqref{eq:tailcond},
\emph{exterior Lipschitz data $(\sigma=1)$ suffices}, and the constant-mode
hypothesis \textnormal{(CM)} of Lemma~\ref{lem:truncdiff} holds.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
For $|y|>R_k\sim 1/r_k$ one has $r_ky\notin B_1$, so the exterior modulus gives
$|w_k^h(y)|=\theta_k^{-1}r_k^{-\beta}|u_k(r_ky+r_kh)-u_k(r_ky)|\le
C|h|^{\sigma}\theta_k^{-1}r_k^{\sigma-\beta}$. With
$|K_{k,h}(0,y)|\lesssim|v_k(y)|^{p-2}\le(M\theta_k^{-1}r_k^{-\beta})^{p-2}$ and
$\int_{|y|>R_k}|y|^{-n-sp}dy\sim r_k^{sp}$,
\[
  |a_k(h)|\lesssim |h|^{\sigma}\,\theta_k^{-1}r_k^{\sigma-\beta}\cdot
   \theta_k^{-(p-2)}r_k^{-\beta(p-2)}\cdot r_k^{sp}
   =|h|^{\sigma}\,\theta_k^{-(p-1)}\,r_k^{\,\sigma-\delta}.\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:exterior}
Lemma~\ref{lem:exterior} turns the stability obstruction into a
\emph{checkable hypothesis on the data}: any solution whose exterior values are
Lipschitz (e.g.\ $u$ globally Lipschitz, or the exterior datum chosen smooth)
satisfies \textnormal{(CM)}, so for such solutions compactness \emph{and}
stability are in place, and the only remaining input of
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} is the Liouville step.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:nocompact}
Known interior H\"older estimates for fractional $p$-Laplacian equations
contain tail terms. Therefore \emph{local $L^\infty$-bounds alone are not
enough to obtain compactness of $(v_k)$}: by
Proposition~\ref{prop:tailobstruction} the tail term entering such estimates
diverges, so the sequence is not, a priori, precompact even in
$C^0_{loc}$. Any compactness statement must either supply uniform tail control
or replace $v_k$ by a modified (truncated or differenced) sequence; we do
\emph{not} assume compactness silently. This is the content of
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}.
\end{remark}

\section{Affine subtraction and shifted kernels: perturbative compactness}
\label{sec:shifted}
%======================================================================
For $p=2$, subtracting an affine function from a solution produces a solution;
for $p\neq2$ it does not. The correct mechanism is to move the affine part
\emph{into the operator}. For $b\in\R^n$ define, formally,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Ab}
  A_b v(x):=\mathrm{P.V.}\!\int_{\R^n}
   J_p\big(v(x)-v(y)+b\cdot(x-y)\big)\,\frac{dy}{|x-y|^{n+sp}},
  \qquad J_p(t)=\Jp{t},
\end{equation}
so that if $\fpl{v}=f$ and $\ell(x)=a+b\cdot x$, then $w=v-\ell$ satisfies
$A_bw=f$. The operator $A_b$ is \emph{monotone}, since for fixed $\tau\in\R$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mono}
  \big(J_p(\xi+\tau)-J_p(\eta+\tau)\big)(\xi-\eta)\ \ge\ 0,
\end{equation}
$J_p$ being nondecreasing. This section proves the compactness/limit-equation
half of the perturbative scheme for the family $\{A_b\}_{|b|\le B}$; the
regularity half is isolated as Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:P2} below and is \emph{not}
claimed.

\subsection{The compensated weak formulation}
The expression \eqref{eq:Ab} requires care: for bounded $v$, the integrand
behaves like $J_p(b\cdot(x-y))\sim|b|^{p-1}|x-y|^{p-1}$ in the far field,
which is \emph{not} absolutely integrable against $|x-y|^{-n-sp}$ when
$p-1\ge sp$. The affine part must be removed by symmetry before absolute
estimates are made. We fix the following convention.

\begin{definition}[Compensated weak solution]\label{def:compweak}
Let $v\in W^{s,p}(B_2)\cap L^\infty(B_2)$ admit the far-field decomposition
$v=g-\ell_b$ on $\R^n\setminus B_2$ with $g\in L^{p-1}_{sp}(\R^n)$
\textnormal{(}finite tail\textnormal{)} and $\ell_b(x)=b\cdot x$. For
$\varphi\in C_c^\infty(B_1)$ set
\begin{align}
  \mathcal E_b(v,\varphi):={}&\frac12\iint_{\{|x-y|\le1\}}
    J_p\big(v(x)-v(y)+b\cdot(x-y)\big)\,
    \frac{(\varphi(x)-\varphi(y))}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,dx\,dy
    \label{eq:comp-near}\\
  &+\int_{\R^n}\varphi(x)\!\int_{\{|x-y|>1\}}
    \Big[J_p\big(v(x)-v(y)+b\cdot(x-y)\big)-J_p\big(b\cdot(x-y)\big)\Big]
    \frac{dy}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,dx,
    \label{eq:comp-far}
\end{align}
where we record that
$\int_{\{|x-y|>1\}}J_p(b\cdot(x-y))\,|x-y|^{-n-sp}\,dy=0$ for every $x$, as an
improper symmetric limit over annuli $\{1<|x-y|<R\}$, $R\to\infty$
\textnormal{(}the integrand is odd in $y-x$ and each annulus integral vanishes
exactly\textnormal{)}. We say $A_bv=f$ \emph{weakly in $B_1$} if
$\mathcal E_b(v,\varphi)=\int f\varphi$ for all such $\varphi$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Well-posedness of the formulation in the range
$p<\frac2{1-s}$]\label{lem:wellposed}
Under the hypotheses of Definition~\ref{def:compweak}, both integrals
\eqref{eq:comp-near}--\eqref{eq:comp-far} converge absolutely, provided
\begin{equation}\label{eq:range-shifted}
  p-2<sp,\qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad p<\frac{2}{1-s}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
\emph{Near region \eqref{eq:comp-near}.} Here $|\varphi(x)-\varphi(y)|\le
\|\nabla\varphi\|_\infty|x-y|$ and, splitting the $J_p$ term by
$|J_p(t)|\le|t|^{p-1}$ and $(a+b)^{p-1}\le c(a^{p-1}+b^{p-1})$,
\[
  \big|J_p(v(x)-v(y)+b\cdot(x-y))\big|
  \le c\,|v(x)-v(y)|^{p-1}+c\,B^{p-1}|x-y|^{p-1}.
\]
The second term contributes $c\iint_{|x-y|\le1}|x-y|^{p-n-sp}$, finite since
$p>sp$. The first is handled by H\"older with exponents $(\tfrac{p}{p-1},p)$
against $|\varphi(x)-\varphi(y)|$, giving the bound
$C[v]_{W^{s,p}}^{p-1}[\varphi]_{W^{s,p}}$ after distributing the kernel;
this is the standard estimate for the unshifted form and does not use
\eqref{eq:range-shifted}.

\emph{Far region \eqref{eq:comp-far}.} By the fundamental theorem of calculus
for $J_p\in C^1$ \textnormal{(}$p\ge2$\textnormal{)},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Jp-diff}
  \big|J_p(t+\tau)-J_p(\tau)\big|\ \le\ (p-1)\,(|t|+|\tau|)^{p-2}\,|t| .
\end{equation}
With $t=v(x)-v(y)$ and $\tau=b\cdot(x-y)$, $|x-y|>1$: for $x\in
B_1=\operatorname{supp}\varphi$ we have $|\tau|\le B|x-y|$ and, by the
far-field decomposition, $|t|\le \|v\|_{L^\infty(B_2)}+|g(y)|+B|y|\cdot
\chi_{\{y\notin B_2\}}$ --- however the affine part of $v(y)$ recombines with
$\tau$: indeed for $y\notin B_2$,
\[
  v(x)-v(y)+b\cdot(x-y)=v(x)-g(y)+b\cdot x,
\]
so the argument of the first $J_p$ in \eqref{eq:comp-far} is bounded by
$C+|g(y)|$, while the reference term $J_p(b\cdot(x-y))$ obeys
\eqref{eq:Jp-diff} with $t$ replaced by $v(x)-g(y)+b\cdot y$
\textnormal{(}write $b\cdot(x-y)=(v(x)-g(y)+b\cdot x)-(v(x)-g(y)+b\cdot y)$
--- we estimate instead directly\textnormal{)}:
\[
  \big|J_p(v(x)-g(y)+b\cdot x)-J_p(b\cdot(x-y))\big|
  \le (p-1)\big(C+|g(y)|+B|x-y|\big)^{p-2}\big(C+|g(y)|+B(1+|x|)\big).
\]
For $|x-y|>1$, $x\in B_1$, the dominant growth in $y$ is
$|x-y|^{p-2}(1+|g(y)|)$, so the $y$-integral is bounded by
\[
  C\int_{|x-y|>1}\frac{|x-y|^{p-2}\,(1+|g(y)|)}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,dy
  \;\le\;
  C\int_{|z|>1}|z|^{\,p-2-n-sp}\,dz
  \;+\;
  C\Big(\int|g|^{p-1}\omega\Big)^{\frac1{p-1}}
   \Big(\int_{|z|>1}|z|^{\frac{(p-2)(p-1)}{p-2}-n-sp}dz\Big)^{\frac{p-2}{p-1}},
\]
and both integrals are finite \emph{precisely} when $p-2<sp$
\textnormal{(}the H\"older-split second factor requires
$p-1<sp+\frac{sp+n}{p-1}$-type conditions implied by it; for $g$ bounded the
first term alone suffices under $p-2<sp$\textnormal{)}. For $y\in
B_2\setminus B_1$-type intermediate regions the integrand is bounded by local
norms. This is where the restriction \eqref{eq:range-shifted} enters, and it
is the \emph{same} restriction under which the homogeneous $C^{1,\al}$
theory~\cite{GJS} and the Lipschitz baseline~\cite{BT} hold: the range
$p<\frac2{1-s}$ appears independently three times \textnormal{(}order of the
linearized operator, GJS, and here\textnormal{)}, and we regard it as the
natural habitat of the theory.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[No pointwise operator is used]\label{rem:no-pointwise}
We never evaluate \eqref{eq:Ab} pointwise: all statements below are in the
sense of Definition~\ref{def:compweak}. The odd-symmetry convention for the
reference term is exactly the compensation that makes bounded functions
admissible for $A_b$; it is the nonlinear analogue of the
polynomial-compensated definitions available for $(-\Delta)^s$
\textnormal{(}\cite{DSV}, linear only\textnormal{)}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Perturbative compactness}

\begin{proposition}[Compactness and the limit equation for shifted
operators]\label{prop:P1}
Let $2\le p<\frac2{1-s}$ and $B\ge0$. Let $b_k\in\R^n$, $|b_k|\le B$,
$b_k\to b$, and let $v_k$ satisfy, in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:compweak},
\[
  A_{b_k}v_k=f_k\quad\text{weakly in }B_1,
  \qquad \|f_k\|_{L^\infty(B_1)}\to0,
\]
with the uniform bounds
\[
  \|v_k\|_{L^\infty(B_2)}\le C_0,\qquad
  [v_k]_{W^{s,p}(B_2)}\le C_0,\qquad
  \|v_k\|_{C^{0,1}(B_{3/2})}\le C_0,
\]
and far-field decompositions $v_k=g_k-\ell_{b_k}$ on $\R^n\setminus B_2$
satisfying \emph{either}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item[\textnormal{(E1)}] shared exterior data: $g_k\equiv g$ with
  $g\in L^{p-1}_{sp}(\R^n)$; \emph{or}
\item[\textnormal{(E2)}] tail convergence: $g_k\to g_0$ a.e.\ on
  $\R^n\setminus B_2$ and in $L^{p-1}_{sp}$, i.e.
  $\int_{\R^n\setminus B_2}\frac{|g_k-g_0|^{p-1}}{1+|y|^{n+sp}}\,dy\to0$,
  with $\sup_k\Tail(g_k;0,2)<\infty$.
\end{itemize}
Then, along a subsequence,
\[
  v_k\to v_0\ \text{locally uniformly in }B_{3/2},
  \qquad
  v_k\rightharpoonup v_0\ \text{weakly in }W^{s,p}_{loc}(B_{3/2}),
\]
and the limit satisfies
\[
  A_bv_0=0\quad\text{weakly in }B_1 .
\]
No $C^1_{loc}$ convergence is used or asserted, and no strong
$W^{s,p}_{loc}$ convergence is claimed.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Step 1: compactness.} The uniform Lipschitz bound on $B_{3/2}$ and
Arzel\`a--Ascoli give $v_k\to v_0$ locally uniformly along a subsequence; the
uniform Gagliardo bound gives $v_k\rightharpoonup v_0$ weakly in
$W^{s,p}(B_r)$ for every $r<2$ \textnormal{(}reflexivity; the two limits
agree a.e.\textnormal{)}. Under (E2), define $v_0:=g_0-\ell_b$ outside $B_2$;
under (E1), $v_0:=g-\ell_b$ there. In either case $v_0$ satisfies the
hypotheses of Definition~\ref{def:compweak}.

\emph{Step 2: the Minty device.} Fix $\varphi\in C_c^\infty(B_1)$ and $t>0$,
and set $V:=v_0+t\varphi$. By the monotonicity \eqref{eq:mono} applied
pointwise in $(x,y)$ with $\tau=b_k\cdot(x-y)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:minty}
  \mathcal E_{b_k}(V,\,V-v_k)\ \ge\ \mathcal E_{b_k}(v_k,\,V-v_k)
  \ =\ \int_{B_1} f_k\,(V-v_k)\,\zeta\,dx\ \longrightarrow\ 0,
\end{equation}
where the pairing is localized by a cutoff $\zeta$ as usual; the right-hand
side vanishes since $\|f_k\|_\infty\to0$ and $V-v_k$ is uniformly bounded on
$B_1$. \textnormal{(}The admissibility of $V-v_k$ as a test function follows
by density from the uniform $W^{s,p}(B_2)\cap L^\infty$ bounds; this is the
standard approximation step and uses no new estimate.\textnormal{)}

\emph{Step 3: passing to the limit in the left-hand side of
\eqref{eq:minty}.} The first argument $V$ is \emph{fixed}; the functional
$\mathcal E_{b_k}(V,\cdot)$ is \emph{linear} in its second argument. Split as
in Definition~\ref{def:compweak}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \emph{Near region.} The density
$G_k(x,y):=J_p(V(x)-V(y)+b_k\cdot(x-y))$ satisfies
$|G_k|\le C|x-y|^{p-1}$ \textnormal{(}$V$ Lipschitz on $B_2$\textnormal{)},
hence $G_k|x-y|^{-\frac{n+sp}{p'}}$ is bounded in
$L^{p'}(\{|x-y|\le1\})$ with $p'=\frac{p}{p-1}$, since
$(p-1)p'=p$ and $\iint_{|x-y|\le1}|x-y|^{p-n-sp}<\infty$. Moreover
$G_k\to G$ pointwise \textnormal{(}$b_k\to b$; $J_p$
continuous\textnormal{)}, hence strongly in the same weighted $L^{p'}$ by
dominated convergence. The second factor
$(V-v_k)(x)-(V-v_k)(y)$, weighted by $|x-y|^{-\frac{n+sp}{p}}$, converges
\emph{weakly} in $L^p(\{|x-y|\le1\}\cap(B_2\times B_2))$ by Step~1. A
strong--weak pairing passes to the limit.
\item \emph{Far region.} For $x\in B_1$ the inner integrand is the
compensated density, bounded by \eqref{eq:Jp-diff} and
Lemma~\ref{lem:wellposed} by a fixed majorant
$\Phi(x,y)\in L^1(\{|x-y|>1\})$ depending only on $C_0$, $B$, $g$-tails
\textnormal{(}uniform under (E1)/(E2)\textnormal{)}; the integrand converges
pointwise a.e.\ by Step~1, (E1)/(E2) and $b_k\to b$. Dominated convergence
applies. \emph{This is the only place the exterior hypothesis is used, and it
cannot be dispensed with}: without (E1)/(E2), far-field mass can create a
spurious right-hand side in the limit \textnormal{(}the counterexample of
\cite[Rem.~4.4]{CSapprox} adapts\textnormal{)}.
\end{itemize}
Therefore $\mathcal E_{b_k}(V,V-v_k)\to\mathcal E_b(V,t\varphi)$, and
\eqref{eq:minty} gives $\mathcal E_b(v_0+t\varphi,\varphi)\ge0$ after
dividing by $t>0$.

\emph{Step 4: hemicontinuity.} As $t\downarrow0$,
$J_p(v_0(x)-v_0(y)+t(\varphi(x)-\varphi(y))+b\cdot(x-y))\to
J_p(v_0(x)-v_0(y)+b\cdot(x-y))$ pointwise, dominated on the near region by
$C|x-y|^{p-1}$ and on the far region by the majorant of
Lemma~\ref{lem:wellposed} \textnormal{(}uniformly for $t\le1$\textnormal{)}.
Hence $\mathcal E_b(v_0+t\varphi,\varphi)\to\mathcal E_b(v_0,\varphi)\ge0$.
Replacing $\varphi$ by $-\varphi$ gives $\mathcal E_b(v_0,\varphi)=0$, i.e.\
$A_bv_0=0$ weakly in $B_1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What Proposition~\ref{prop:P1} does and does not use]
The proof uses: local uniform convergence, weak $W^{s,p}_{loc}$ compactness,
monotonicity, and dominated far-field convergence. It does \emph{not} use:
strong $W^{s,p}$ convergence, any $C^1$ information, or any regularity of the
limit beyond Lipschitz. The Lipschitz hypothesis itself is supplied in
applications by the inhomogeneous baseline~\cite{BT}, which holds in the same
range $p<\frac2{1-s}$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The missing regularity input}

\begin{hypothesis}[Shifted homogeneous $C^{1,\al}$ estimate;
``shifted-GJS input'']\label{hyp:P2}
For every $B>0$ there exist $\al_B\in(0,1)$ and $C_B>0$ such that: if
$|b|\le B$ and $A_bv=0$ weakly in $B_1$ \textnormal{(}in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:compweak}\textnormal{)}, with
$\|v\|_{L^\infty(B_2)}\le1$ and normalized far-field data
$\Tail(g;0,2)\le1$, then
\[
  \|v\|_{C^{1,\al_B}(B_{1/2})}\ \le\ C_B .
\]
\end{hypothesis}

\noindent\emph{Status.} For $b=0$ this is precisely the interior estimate
of~\cite{GJS} \textnormal{(}Theorem~1.1\textnormal{)}. For $b\neq0$ it is
\emph{expected} to follow from the proof in~\cite{GJS}: their second and
third stages analyze precisely the regime where the gradient is close to a
fixed nonzero vector \textnormal{(}Ishii--Lions applied to $u-e\cdot x$;
linearized H\"older estimates under nondegeneracy\textnormal{)}, which is the
$b\neq0$ regime after adding back the affine function. However, \cite{GJS}
state their theorem only for $(-\Delta_p)^su=0$, and the affine function
itself has infinite $\Tail$ when $sp\le p-1$, so Theorem~1.1 \emph{cannot be
cited directly} for $A_b$ with $b\neq0$. We therefore record
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:P2} as an input --- an extraction from the methods
of~\cite{GJS}, not from their statement.

\begin{corollary}[Compactness toward regular shifted profiles]\label{cor:P1P2}
Assume Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:P2}. In the setting of
Proposition~\ref{prop:P1}, the limit $v_0$ satisfies
$\|v_0\|_{C^{1,\al_B}(B_{1/2})}\le C_B$, and consequently, for every
$\varepsilon>0$ there is $k_0$ such that for $k\ge k_0$
\[
  \|v_k-v_0\|_{L^\infty(B_{1/2})}\le\varepsilon,
  \qquad v_0\in C^{1,\al_B}(B_{1/2}),\ A_bv_0=0 .
\]
That is: sequences with vanishing right-hand sides are compact, in
$L^\infty(B_{1/2})$, toward shifted homogeneous profiles enjoying full
$C^{1,\al}$ regularity. \textnormal{(}The closeness is in $L^\infty$, not
$C^1$: a $C^1$-closeness claim would presuppose gradient equicontinuity of
$v_k$, which is not available at this stage.\textnormal{)}
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Why this matters for the blow-up scheme}
In the perturbative (excess-decay) iteration, one approximates the blow-up
$v$ at scale $\rho^j$ by affine functions $\ell_j$ and studies
$w_j:=(v-\ell_j)(\rho^j\cdot)/\rho^{j(1+\al')}$. For $p\neq2$ the function
$w_j$ does \emph{not} solve the original equation; it solves
$A_{b_j}w_j=f_j$ with $b_j$ the accumulated affine slope and $f_j$ inheriting
smallness from the dead-core right-hand side
\textnormal{(}Proposition~\ref{prop:homog}\textnormal{)} \emph{and} from the
subcritical normalization: since $1+\al'\le\frac{sp}{p-1}$, the rescalings
remain tail-admissible, in contrast with every attempt at the supercritical
exponent $\beta$. Proposition~\ref{prop:P1} supplies exactly the
compactness/limit-identification for this iteration, uniformly in the shift;
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:P2} supplies the decay of the limiting profile that the
iteration transfers back to $w_j$. Together they would give uniform
$C^{1,\al'}$ estimates for blow-ups with vanishing right-hand side --- the
input denoted ``$C^1_{loc}$-compactness'' in
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact} --- and thereby also the seminorm normalization
that feeds the unit-scale nondegeneracy mechanism of the Liouville section.

\begin{remark}[Limitations: exactly what remains unproved here]
\label{rem:P1-limits}
\textnormal{(i)} Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:P2} is unproved; its expected proof is
an adaptation, not a citation, of~\cite{GJS}. \textnormal{(ii)} The
excess-decay iteration sketched above, including the bookkeeping of the
accumulated shifts $b_j$ and of the inherited right-hand sides $f_j$, is not
carried out in this paper. \textnormal{(iii)} Strong $W^{s,p}_{loc}$
convergence of $v_k$, while plausibly available through the
$(S_+)$-property, is neither proved nor used. \textnormal{(iv)} Nothing in
this section addresses the degenerate-kernel Liouville
\textnormal{(}Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:L}\textnormal{)}, which remains the
principal open input of the main theorem.
\end{remark}

\section{The Liouville step}\label{sec:liouville}
%======================================================================
The blow-up limit, if it exists and solves $\fpl{v_0}=0$, must be classified
among entire $(s,p)$-harmonic functions with a vanishing first jet. Both
statements below are \emph{conditional}.

\subsection{Direct rescaling: a global tail hypothesis}
\begin{lemma}[Conditional Liouville, direct route]\label{lem:liouville-cond}
Let $v$ be $(s,p)$-harmonic in $\R^n$ with $v(0)=|Dv(0)|=0$ and
$\beta<1+\al_0$. Suppose the rescalings $v_R(x):=v(Rx)/R^{\beta}$, $R\ge1$,
satisfy the uniform admissibility bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tail-assumption}
  \|v_R\|_{L^\infty(B_2)}+\Tail(v_R;0,2)\le M_0\qquad(R\ge1).
\end{equation}
Then $v\equiv0$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Each $v_R$ is $(s,p)$-harmonic. By Lemma~\ref{lem:gjs} and
\eqref{eq:tail-assumption}, $\|v_R\|_{C^{1,\al_0}(B_1)}\le CM_0$; since
$v_R(0)=|Dv_R(0)|=0$, $|v_R(x)|\le CM_0|x|^{1+\al_0}$ on $B_1$. For fixed $z$
and $R>2|z|$, $\frac{|v(z)|}{R^{\beta}}=|v_R(z/R)|\le CM_0|z/R|^{1+\al_0}$, so
$|v(z)|\le CM_0|z|^{1+\al_0}R^{\beta-(1+\al_0)}\to0$ since $\beta<1+\al_0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why \eqref{eq:tail-assumption} is not cosmetic]\label{rem:tail}
A pointwise growth bound $|v(x)|\le C(1+|x|)^{\beta}$ does \emph{not} imply
\eqref{eq:tail-assumption}: the tail of $v_R$ scales like
$\int^\infty R^{\beta(p-1)-sp-1}dR$, finite only if $\beta(p-1)<sp$, whereas
$\beta(p-1)-sp=\frac{\gamma sp}{p-1-\gamma}>0$ for every $\gamma>0$. So the
direct route genuinely requires \eqref{eq:tail-assumption}. In the linear case
$p=2$ one bypasses it by working with differences, whose growth is one order
lower; for $p\neq2$ differences do not solve the homogeneous equation, the
obstruction addressed next.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Increment route: a conditional Liouville lemma}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:linincr}, $w^h=v(\cdot+h)-v$ solves the linear
equation $L_{K_h}w^h=0$; its growth is one order below that of $v$. The
relevant tail for this \emph{linear} equation carries the kernel coefficient
$K_h(x,y)\sim|v(y)|^{p-2}$ in the far field, so finiteness requires
$\beta(p-1)<sp+1$, not $(\beta-1)(p-1)<sp$.

\begin{remark}[The increment tail condition]\label{rem:tailcond}
For $|w^h(y)|\sim|y|^{\beta-1}$ and $K_h(x,y)|y|^{-n-sp}\sim
|y|^{\beta(p-2)-n-sp}$, the integral $\int_{|y|>1}|w^h(y)|K_h(x,y)|y|^{-n-sp}dy$
converges iff $(\beta-1)+\beta(p-2)-sp<0$, i.e.\ $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$, which is
\eqref{eq:tailcond}: $\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}$. This is consistent with the
$\mathrm{Tail}_{p-1,sp+1}$ that appears in the linearized estimates of~\cite{GJS}.
\end{remark}

The missing global input deserves a name and a precise statement.

\begin{hypothesis}[Degenerate-kernel Liouville property]\label{hyp:L}
Let $v_0$ be an entire function in the admissible class \textnormal{(}growth
$|v_0(x)|\le C(1+|x|)^{\beta}$, tail condition \eqref{eq:tailcond}\textnormal{)}
and let $L_{0,h}$ denote the linearized operator with kernel
$K_0^h(x,y)=a_0^h(x,y)|x-y|^{-n-sp}$, where $a_0^h$ is the increment
coefficient of Corollary~\ref{cor:incr-weak} built from $v_0$. \emph{We say
that Hypothesis \textnormal{(L)} holds if}: whenever $W\in C^0(\R^n)$
satisfies, weakly,
\[
  L_{0,h}W=0\ \text{ in }\R^n,
  \qquad |W(x)|\le C\,(1+|x|)^{\delta}\ \text{ for some }0<\delta<1,
\]
then $W$ is constant.
\end{hypothesis}

\noindent The coefficient $a_0^h$ may vanish (Remark~\ref{rem:order}), so
Hypothesis~(L) does \emph{not} follow from the uniformly elliptic nonlocal
Liouville theory \cite{FRO,Serra}; it is exactly the statement those theories
would give if uniform ellipticity held. The scale-uniform H\"older hypothesis
$(\star)$ below implies Hypothesis~(L) by the rescaling argument in the proof
of Theorem~\ref{thm:liouville-incr}; conversely (L) is the minimal form needed
to close the contradiction scheme of \S\ref{sec:growth}.

\begin{theorem}[Conditional Liouville, increment route]\label{thm:liouville-incr}
Let $p\ge2$, $s>1-\frac2p$, and let $v\in C^{1,\al_0}_{loc}(\R^n)$ be entire
$(s,p)$-harmonic with $v(0)=|Dv(0)|=0$, growth $|v(x)|\le C(1+|x|)^{\beta}$ and
$|Dv(x)|\le C(1+|x|)^{\beta-1}$, with $1<\beta<1+\asp$ and
$\beta(p-1)<sp+1$. Assume:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item[\textnormal{(ND)}] $v$ is \emph{nondegenerate}: $Dv\not\equiv0$ on any
open set, and on every compact subset of $\{Dv\neq0\}$ the kernel $K_h$ is
uniformly elliptic of order $sp-p+2$;
\item[$(\star)$] the local H\"older estimate of Lemma~\ref{lem:gjs} for
$L_{K_h}$ holds \emph{scale-uniformly}: there is $\asp\in(0,1)$ such that, for
the rescalings $w_R:=w^h(R\cdot)/R^{\beta-1}$ and centers in $\{Dv\neq0\}$,
$[w_R]_{C^{\asp}(B_{1/2})}\le C$ uniformly in $R\ge1$.
\end{itemize}
Then every increment $w^h$ is constant; hence $v$ is affine, and the vanishing
jet gives $v\equiv0$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix $h$ and write $w=w^h$, $\beta'=\beta-1<\asp$. By $(\star)$,
$[w]_{C^{\asp}(B_{R/2})}=R^{\beta'-\asp}[w_R]_{C^{\asp}(B_{1/2})}\le
CR^{\beta'-\asp}\to0$ as $R\to\infty$, since $\beta'<\asp$. Hence
$[w]_{C^{\asp}(\R^n)}=0$, so $w$ is constant. (No translation invariance of the
kernel is used in this step; (ND) is what makes $(\star)$ meaningful across
centers.) Thus $v(\cdot+h)-v\equiv c(h)$; the cocycle identity
$c(h_1+h_2)=c(h_1)+c(h_2)$ and continuity give $c(h)=b\cdot h$, while
$c(h)=v(h)$ has a double zero at $0$, so $b=0$ and $v\equiv$ const $=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The cap is $\beta<1+\asp$, not $\beta<2$]\label{rem:incr-cap}
Two points must be stated honestly. First, $(\star)$ is the genuinely missing
input: \cite{BLS,GJS} prove the \emph{local} H\"older estimate
(Lemma~\ref{lem:gjs}) and use it to bootstrap the regularity of a given
solution, but they do \emph{not} prove the global, scale-uniform Liouville
property used above. Second, with only a $C^{\asp}$ (H\"older) estimate
available for the linearized equation, the rescaling forces the cap
$\beta-1<\asp$, i.e.\ $\beta<1+\asp$. The stronger cap $\beta<2$ would require a
$C^{1,\asp}$ estimate for the increment --- equivalently $C^{2,\asp}$ for the
$p$-Laplacian --- which does not exist for $p\neq2$. The value $\beta<2$ is
special to $p=2$, where the increment is $s$-harmonic and smooth. We therefore
write the cap as $\beta<1+\asp$ throughout.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Nonemptiness]\label{rem:nonempty}
Under \eqref{eq:gjs-range} the constraints $\beta>1$, $\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}$,
and $\beta<1+\asp$ (any $\asp>0$) define a nonempty $\gamma$-interval
\[
  \max\Big\{0,\,p-1-sp\Big\}<\gamma<
   \min\Big\{\tfrac{p-1}{1+sp},\ (p-1)-\tfrac{sp}{1+\asp}\Big\},
\]
and the interval is nonempty \emph{iff} $s>1-\frac2p$ (equivalently
$p<\frac2{1-s}$). Indeed $\big(\tfrac{p-1}{1+sp}-(p-1-sp)\big)(1+sp)
=sp\,(sp-p+2)$, positive iff $sp-p+2>0$. At $p=2$ the tail condition is
$\gamma<\frac1{1+2s}$, matching~\cite{PTU}.
\end{remark}

\section{The conditional growth estimate}\label{sec:growth}
%======================================================================
\begin{definition}[Flat branching point]\label{def:branch}
A point $x_0\in B_1$ is a \emph{flat branching point} of $u$ if $u(x_0)=0$ and
$u$ vanishes to first order at $x_0$, i.e.\ $\sup_{B_r(x_0)}|u|=o(r)$ as
$r\to0$. When $u$ is of class $C^1$ near $x_0$ --- which, for the inhomogeneous
equation, is presently available only conditionally (Remark~\ref{rem:baseline})
--- this is equivalent to $u(x_0)=|Du(x_0)|=0$.
\end{definition}

\begin{assumption}[Compactness and admissibility]\label{ass:compact}
Solutions of \eqref{eq:main} are of class $C^{1+\al'}_{loc}$ for some
$\al'\in(0,\al_0)$, with uniform interior bounds under the normalizations
below \textnormal{(}an inhomogeneous $C^{1,\al'}$ input,
Remark~\ref{rem:baseline}\textnormal{)}. Moreover, whenever amplified blow-ups
$v_k$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:homog}) satisfy $\sup_{B_R}|v_k|\le CR^{\beta}$ for
$1\le R\le c/r_k$ and are nontrivial on $B_1$, a subsequence converges locally
uniformly to an entire $v_0$ whose increments satisfy $L_{K}w_0^h=0$ (the
renormalized limiting equation, Remark~\ref{rem:renorm}), with
$v_0(0)=|Dv_0(0)|=0$, and
$v_0$ admissible for the Liouville step (Lemma~\ref{lem:liouville-cond} or
Theorem~\ref{thm:liouville-incr}, including its hypotheses \textnormal{(ND)}
and $(\star)$).
\end{assumption}

\begin{theorem}[Conditional growth estimate]\label{thm:growth}
Assume the parameter block \eqref{eq:hyp-block} and
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}. Let $u\in L^\infty(\R^n)$ solve \eqref{eq:main}
with $0$ a flat branching point. Then
\[
  |u(x)|\le C\,|x|^{\beta},\qquad x\in B_{1/2},\qquad C=C(n,s,p,\gamma).
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix $\al'\in(0,\min\{\al_0,\beta-1\})$, nonempty since $\beta>1$ and
$\al_0>0$ (here the $C^{1,\al'}$ regularity is part of
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}). Suppose the estimate fails; then there are
normalized solutions $u_k$ with $u_k(0)=|Du_k(0)|=0$,
$[u_k]_{C^{1+\al'}(B_{1/2})}\le 2C$, and points $x_k$ with
$|u_k(x_k)|>k|x_k|^{\beta}$. With
$\theta_k(r'):=\sup_{r'<r<1/2}r^{1+\al'-\beta}[u_k]_{C^{1+\al'}(B_r)}$
($1+\al'-\beta<0$), the jet-to-growth lemma forces $\theta_k(0^+)>k$; choose
$r_k>1/k$ with
$r_k^{1+\al'-\beta}[u_k]_{C^{1+\al'}(B_{r_k})}\ge\frac1k\theta_k(1/k)\ge
\frac12\theta_k(r_k)$, so $r_k\to0$. Set
$v_k:=u_k(r_k\cdot)/(\theta_k(r_k)r_k^{\beta})$. As in~\cite[(33)]{CP},
$[v_k]_{C^{1+\al'}(B_R)}\le R^{\beta-1-\al'}$ for $1\le R\le\frac1{2r_k}$, and
the gradient-anchor argument gives $|v_k(x)|\le C|x|^{\beta}$ on that range with
$[v_k]_{C^{1+\al'}(B_1)}\ge\frac12$. By Assumption~\ref{ass:compact},
$v_k\to v_0$ locally uniformly, the increments of $v_0$ satisfy $L_K w_0^h=0$
(Remark~\ref{rem:renorm}), $v_0(0)=|Dv_0(0)|=0$,
$|v_0(x)|\le C(1+|x|)^{\beta}$, $[v_0]_{C^{1+\al'}(B_1)}\ge\frac12$. Since
$\beta<1+\asp$ (and $\beta<1+\al_0$), the Liouville step
(Lemma~\ref{lem:liouville-cond}, or Theorem~\ref{thm:liouville-incr} under
\textnormal{(ND)} and $(\star)$) gives $v_0\equiv0$, contradicting the
normalization.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The proof runs the blow-up at the \emph{generic} exponent $\al'$, strictly
below $\al_0$; the sharp exponents enter only through the regularity inputs.
The two genuinely nonlinear-nonlocal difficulties are isolated as
Assumption~\ref{ass:compact} (compactness/tails) and the hypotheses
\textnormal{(ND)}, $(\star)$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:liouville-incr}.
\end{remark}

\section{Consequences}\label{sec:consequences}
%======================================================================
\begin{corollary}[Gradient growth, conditional]\label{cor:grad}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}, and provided the
inhomogeneous $C^{1,\al'}$ bound of Assumption~\ref{ass:compact} is available,
$\sup_{B_r}|Du|\le Cr^{\beta-1}$ for $r<\frac12$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[Local limit $s\nearrow1$, conditional]\label{thm:local}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} hold for $s$ near $1$ with
constants uniform in $s$, and let $u$ solve
$\Delta_p u=u_+^\gamma-u_-^\gamma$ in $B_1$ with $0$ a flat branching point.
Then $|u(x)|\le C|x|^{p/(p-1-\gamma)}$ in $B_{1/2}$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof (sketch)]
As $s\nearrow1$, $\fpl{u}\to-\Delta_p u$ after fixing normalizing constants and
$\beta\to\frac{p}{p-1-\gamma}$; passing to the limit in
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth} gives the claim. The uniformity in $s$ is itself
conditional: the constants of Lemma~\ref{lem:gjs} are \emph{not} uniform as
$s\to1$, so the honest route to the local result is a direct one (the scheme
of~\cite[\S6]{CP} with classical $C^{1,\al}$ theory for $\Delta_p$), to be
carried out separately.
\end{proof}

\subsection{A scale-local barrier}\label{ssec:barrier}
The non-admissibility of $|x|^{\beta}$ as a global profile ($\beta(p-1)>sp$) is,
for barriers, an \emph{advantage}: a power capped far away retains a large
negative tail contribution that fixes the sign of the operator on a fixed
annulus.

\begin{lemma}[Scale-local barrier]\label{lem:barrier}
Let $p\ge2$, $s\in(0,1)$, $0<\gamma<p-1$, $\beta=\frac{sp}{p-1-\gamma}>1$, so
$\beta(p-1)-sp=\beta\gamma>0$. For $L>1$ let $\psi_L:\R^n\to[0,\infty)$ be radial,
nondecreasing in $|x|$, $\psi_L\in C^2(\R^n\setminus\{0\})\cap L^\infty(\R^n)$,
with
\[
  \psi_L(x)=|x|^{\beta}\ (|x|\le2),\qquad \psi_L(x)=L^{\beta}\ (|x|\ge2L),\qquad
  |x|^{\beta}\le\psi_L(x)\le L^{\beta}\ (2\le|x|\le2L),
\]
the interpolation on $\{2<|x|<2L\}$ being smooth and monotone. Write
$Q_L:=\fpl{\psi_L}$, $\mathcal A:=\{1\le|z|\le\tfrac32\}$. Then:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item[\textnormal{(i)}] $\fpl{[R^{\beta}\psi_L(\cdot/R)]}(x)=R^{\beta\gamma}\,
  Q_L(x/R)$;
\item[\textnormal{(ii)}] $Q_L$ is finite and continuous on $\mathcal A$, and there
  exist $L_0>1$, $c_0>0$ (depending on $n,s,p,\beta$ and the fixed interpolation)
  with $Q_L(z)\le -c_0<0$ on $\mathcal A$ for all $L\ge L_0$;
\item[\textnormal{(iii)}] hence, with $\psi_{L,R}(x):=R^{\beta}\psi_L(x/R)$,
  \[
    \fpl{\psi_{L,R}}(x)\le -c_0\,R^{\beta\gamma}<0,\qquad R\le|x|\le\tfrac32R .
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
(i) As in Proposition~\ref{prop:scaling}, using $\beta(p-1)-sp=\beta\gamma$.

(ii) Fix $z\in\mathcal A$. Since $z\ne0$, $\psi_L\in C^2$ near $z$ and is
bounded, so the principal value converges; continuity in $z$ follows. Split
$Q_L=Q_{\mathrm{sing}}+Q_{\mathrm{far}}$ with $Q_{\mathrm{sing}}=
\mathrm{P.V.}\!\int_{|y-z|<1/4}$ and $Q_{\mathrm{far}}=\int_{|y-z|\ge1/4}$
(absolutely convergent). For $Q_{\mathrm{sing}}$, both $z\pm\zeta\in B_2$ when
$|\zeta|<\tfrac14$, where $\psi_L=|\cdot|^{\beta}$; symmetrizing
$y=z\pm\zeta$ and using $J_p$ odd,
\[
  Q_{\mathrm{sing}}(z)=-\tfrac{c}{2}\!\int_{|\zeta|<1/4}\!\big[J_p(a_\zeta)+
   J_p(b_\zeta)\big]|\zeta|^{-n-sp}d\zeta,\quad a_\zeta+b_\zeta\ge0,
\]
by convexity of $|z|^{\beta}$ ($\beta>1$); $J_p$ nondecreasing then gives
$J_p(a_\zeta)+J_p(b_\zeta)\ge0$, so $Q_{\mathrm{sing}}(z)\le0$. For
$Q_{\mathrm{far}}$ (no singularity), $\psi_L$ nondecreasing gives
$\psi_L(y)\ge\psi_L(z)$ on $\{|y|>|z|\}$, so $J_p(\psi_L(z)-\psi_L(y))\le0$
there; the only positive part is $\{|y-z|\ge\tfrac14\}\cap\{|y|<|z|\}\subset
B_{3/2}$, where $\psi_L=|\cdot|^{\beta}$ and the kernel is $\le 4^{n+sp}$,
contributing at most $P_1=P_1(n,s,p,\beta)$ \emph{independent of $L$}. On
$\{|y|\ge2L\}$, $\psi_L(y)=L^{\beta}$ and, for $L$ large,
$\psi_L(z)\le(\tfrac32)^{\beta}\le\tfrac12L^{\beta}$, so
\[
  \int_{|y|\ge2L}\!J_p(\psi_L(z)-L^{\beta})\,|z-y|^{-n-sp}dy
  \le -\big(\tfrac12L^{\beta}\big)^{p-1}\!\!\int_{|y|\ge2L}\!|z-y|^{-n-sp}dy
  \le -c_1L^{\beta(p-1)}L^{-sp}=-c_1L^{\beta\gamma}.
\]
Hence $Q_L(z)\le Q_{\mathrm{sing}}+P_1-c_1L^{\beta\gamma}\le P_1-c_1
L^{\beta\gamma}$ uniformly on $\mathcal A$; choosing $L_0$ with $c_1L_0^{\beta
\gamma}\ge P_1+c_0$ gives (ii). (iii) is (i) on $\mathcal A$ rescaled.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Amplitude vs.\ reaction; two-phase caveat]\label{rem:barrier}
For $A>0$, $\fpl{(A\psi_{L,R})}=A^{p-1}\fpl{\psi_{L,R}}$ while
$(A\psi_{L,R})_+^{\gamma}=A^{\gamma}R^{\beta\gamma}\psi_L(\cdot/R)^{\gamma}$;
since $p-1>\gamma$, the operator and reaction terms (both of order
$R^{\beta\gamma}$) are separated by the free factor $A^{p-1-\gamma}$, so
$A\psi_{L,R}$ can be made a super- or sub-solution of \eqref{eq:main} at each
scale. The mechanism of Lemma~\ref{lem:barrier} is that the \emph{non-admissible
tail} of $|x|^{\beta}$, once capped at height $L^{\beta}$, contributes
$\sim -L^{\beta\gamma}\to-\infty$, fixing the sign on the fixed annulus.
\textbf{Two-phase caveat.} The comparison principle for $\fpl{}$ is nonlocal and
requires ordering on the entire complement; $\psi_{L,R}\ge0$ controls the
positive phase only, and (by $u\mapsto-u$) the negative phase. We thus obtain
annular nondegeneracy \emph{within each one-phase region under the comparison
hypotheses}, not across a branching point, where the complement ordering is
delicate --- the open step in route (A) of Section~\ref{sec:open}.
\end{remark}

\section{Remaining analytic inputs}\label{sec:open}
%======================================================================
The following are unconditional: the scaling identity
(Proposition~\ref{prop:scaling}), the vanishing of the right-hand side along
amplified blow-ups (Proposition~\ref{prop:homog}), the increment linearization
(Proposition~\ref{prop:linincr}), the tail obstruction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:tailobstruction}), and the contradiction scheme modulo
its inputs (Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}). The growth estimate is conditional on
the following, each a concrete analytic statement rather than a black box. We
emphasize that the principal missing ingredient is \emph{not} local regularity
---which is available (Lemma~\ref{lem:gjs})---but \emph{global rigidity for the
degenerate linearized kernel}; items (3) and (4) together with the compactness
of item (1) are the true bottlenecks.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \emph{Compactness of normalized blow-ups, and the far-field increment
control.} By Proposition~\ref{prop:tailobstruction} the available bounds permit
a divergent tail. Truncation (Lemma~\ref{lem:trunc}) controls the far-field
\emph{oscillation} under $\beta(p-1)<sp+1$ but not its constant; combining
truncation with differencing closes the constant at $p=2$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:p2cancel}) but, for $p\neq2$, the constant $a_k(h)$ is
removed neither by a commutator/cocycle nor by an a posteriori bound
(Proposition~\ref{prop:pneq2}). The precise remaining obstruction is therefore
the \emph{uniform far-field increment control} $a_k(h)\to0$ of
Remark~\ref{rem:farfield}. This last point is \emph{discharged} under a
hypothesis on the exterior data: by Lemma~\ref{lem:exterior}, exterior
$C^{0,\sigma}$ data with $\sigma>\beta(p-1)-sp$ (in particular exterior
Lipschitz data) give $a_k(h)\to0$. So compactness and stability are in place for
solutions with regular exterior data; the obstruction migrates entirely to the
Liouville step.
\item \emph{Control of divergent tails / a stability theorem.} A stability
statement for $\fpl{}$ (resp.\ $L_{K}$) under local uniform convergence with
the appropriate global tail convergence, of the type underlying~\cite{BLS,KKL};
for the constant mode, Lemma~\ref{lem:exterior} suffices under regular exterior
data.
\item \emph{A global, scale-uniform Liouville theorem for the degenerate
linearized kernel} --- the hypothesis $(\star)$ of
Theorem~\ref{thm:liouville-incr}, and the principal remaining problem. The
\emph{local} H\"older estimate is available (\cite[\S7]{GJS,FRO,Serra}, under
uniform ellipticity); the scale-uniform/global form for the \emph{degenerate}
$K_h$ is not. Two structured routes appear more tractable than arbitrary
degeneracy:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item[(W)] \emph{Weighted-kernel model.} Near a flat point $|Dv(x)|\sim
  |x|^{\beta-1}$, so $K_h$ behaves like the \emph{weighted} kernel
  $\mathcal K(x,y)\sim(|x|+|y|)^{a}|x-y|^{-n-m}$ with $m=sp-p+2$ and
  $a=(\beta-1)(p-2)\ge0$. For $a<n$ (i.e.\ $(\beta-1)(p-2)<n$) the weight
  $|x|^a$ is of Muckenhoupt $A_2$ type; proving H\"older/Liouville estimates
  for this weighted class is a concrete, structured problem replacing
  ``arbitrary degenerate kernel''. This route has recently acquired a solid
  foundation: Behn--Diening--Ok--Rolfes~\cite{BDOR} develop interior H\"older
  continuity and Harnack inequalities (their Theorems 3.4--3.6) for nonlocal
  equations whose kernels are comparable to
  $w(x)w(y)/w(B_{x,y})\cdot|x-y|^{-n}$ with a Muckenhoupt $A_p$ weight $w$ ---
  a nonlocal analogue of Fabes--Kenig--Serapioni, with constants stable as
  $s\nearrow1$. The BDOR framework suggests a possible path to the missing
  Liouville theorem (Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:L}), \emph{provided} one can verify a
  suitable Muckenhoupt-type admissibility condition for the linearized
  coefficient $a_0^h$. We emphasize what is \emph{not} automatic: their
  kernels are point-degenerate of product type, whereas $a_0^h$ is
  pair-degenerate ($a_0^h$ small where $v_0(x)\approx v_0(y)$); their theory
  is stated for the homogeneous equation (they indicate the nonhomogeneous
  extension is expected); and their energy is the $p$-form, while $L_{0,h}$ is
  the linearized quadratic form. Verifying an \emph{averaged} comparability
  $a_0^h(x,y)\gtrsim w(x)^{1/2}w(y)^{1/2}$ with $w\sim|Dv_0|^{p-2}$ in the
  $A_2$ sense --- strictly weaker than pointwise nondegeneracy --- is the
  concrete open problem; the case $n=2$, where $|Du|^{p-2}$ is known to be a
  Muckenhoupt weight for local $p$-harmonic $u$ via quasiregularity of the
  gradient, is the natural first target.
\item[(A)] \emph{Annular nondegeneracy.} If the blow-up limit satisfies
  $|Dv(x)|\sim R^{\beta-1}$ on a large portion of each annulus
  $B_{2R}\setminus B_R$, rescaling the annulus to unit size makes $K_h$
  uniformly elliptic, so the \cite{GJS}-type local estimate applies per annulus
  and the decay argument closes the Liouville. The scale-local barrier
  Lemma~\ref{lem:barrier} ($\fpl{\psi_{L,R}}\le-c_0R^{\beta\gamma}$ on
  $R\le|x|\le\frac32R$) is the tool for this, valid \emph{within one phase};
  establishing annular nondegeneracy \emph{across} a branching point
  (Remark~\ref{rem:barrier}) is the remaining step.
\end{itemize}
\item \emph{Degeneracy of the linearized kernel where $Dv=0$.} The kernel
$K_h$ degenerates at critical points of $v$, in particular at the flat origin
of the blow-up limit (Remark~\ref{rem:order}); hence the nondegeneracy
hypothesis \textnormal{(ND)} must be established for $v_0$, e.g.\ via a barrier
computation for $\fpl{|x|^{\beta}}$, or assumed.
\item \emph{Inhomogeneous $C^1$-regularity / flatness.}
B\"ogelein--Duzaar--Liao--Moring~\cite{BDLM} provide Calder\'on--Zygmund-type
gradient \emph{integrability} ($\nabla u\in L^Q_{loc}$ for all finite $Q$ when
$f\in L^\infty$) and higher differentiability $u\in W^{1+\al,p}_{loc}$, but
\emph{not} an interior $C^{1,\al}$ estimate in general --- the embedding
$W^{1+\al,p}\hookrightarrow C^{1,\sigma}$ requires $\al p>n$. Hence the
$C^1$-flatness gap is \emph{not} closed: the condition $Du(x_0)=0$ in
Definition~\ref{def:branch} remains conditional, and the weaker flatness
$\sup_{B_r}|u|=o(r)$ is the right formulation. A natural intermediate target
is a \emph{perturbation lemma}: since the blow-up right-hand sides satisfy
$f_k\to0$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:incr-weak}), a compactness/improvement-of-
flatness argument against the homogeneous estimate of~\cite{GJS} should yield
uniform interior $C^{1,\al'}$ bounds, $\al'<\al_0$, for solutions with
$\|f\|_{L^\infty}$ small --- weaker than a full inhomogeneous $C^{1,\al}$
theory and plausibly within reach, but \emph{it is another input to prove},
not to cite; it would inherit the restriction $p<\frac{2}{1-s}$ and lose
uniformity as $s\nearrow1$. Until it is proved, the $C^1_{loc}$-compactness
of the blow-ups (Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}) must not be assumed silently.
\item \emph{A modulo-polynomial theory for the degenerate increment kernel.}
The blow-up limit has too large a tail to be $(s,p)$-harmonic in the standard
sense (Remark~\ref{rem:renorm}); the right framework is the polynomial-growth
notion of~\cite{DSV}, in which the equation holds \emph{modulo polynomials} and
the far-field constant $a_k$ (degree $0$) is identified with $0$. This resolves
the constant/stability obstruction for $p=2$ (Remark~\ref{rem:dsv}). For
$p\neq2$ one needs the analogue of~\cite{DSV} for the variable, degenerate,
non-translation-invariant kernel $K_h$, in two pieces:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item[(i)] a \emph{modulo-polynomial stability} statement: if
  $L_{K_k}\tilde w_k=f_k+a_k+g_k$ on fixed balls with $f_k,g_k\to0$ and $a_k$
  constant, and $K_k\to K_0$, $\tilde w_k\to w_0$ locally with polynomial
  growth, then $L_{K_0}w_0=0$ modulo constants;
\item[(ii)] a \emph{Liouville modulo constants}: if $L_{K_h}w=0$ modulo
  constants in $\R^n$ with $|w(x)|\le C(1+|x|)^{\beta-1}$, $\beta-1<\asp$, then
  $w$ is constant.
\end{itemize}
Neither is supplied by \cite{DSV,FRO,Serra}, which require uniform ellipticity;
the degeneracy of $K_h$ at $\{Dv=0\}$ is the obstruction common to both.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion: status of each step}\label{sec:conclusion}
%======================================================================
The table below records the status of every step of the program. ``Rigorous''
means proved in this paper; ``literature'' means used with a precise citation;
``conditional'' means proved here modulo a stated assumption; ``open'' means a
genuinely new statement that must be proved before the main theorem becomes
unconditional.

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.35}
\begin{tabular}{|p{0.21\textwidth}|p{0.72\textwidth}|}
\hline
\textbf{Rigorous} &
Scaling identity and the exponent $\beta_*=\frac{sp}{p-1-\gamma}$
(Prop.~\ref{prop:scaling}); vanishing right-hand side along amplified
blow-ups (Prop.~\ref{prop:homog}); weak increment identity and
$f_k^h\to0$ (Prop.~\ref{prop:linincr}, Cor.~\ref{cor:incr-weak}); far-field
tail condition $\beta_*(p-1)<sp+1\iff\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}$, with the
far-field coefficient $\sim|y|^{\beta_*(p-2)}$
(Rem.~\ref{rem:tailcond}); tail obstruction for the normalized sequence
(Prop.~\ref{prop:tailobstruction}); truncation oscillation estimates
(Lem.~\ref{lem:trunc}, \ref{lem:trunc-incr}); constant cancellation at
$p=2$ and its failure mechanisms at $p\neq2$
(Prop.~\ref{prop:p2cancel}, \ref{prop:pneq2}); stability under exterior
H\"older data (Lem.~\ref{lem:exterior}); scale-local barrier
(Lem.~\ref{lem:barrier}); contradiction scheme modulo its inputs
(Thm.~\ref{thm:growth}). \\
\hline
\textbf{Literature} &
Inhomogeneous H\"older/Lipschitz baseline, $p<\frac2{1-s}$~\cite{BT};
homogeneous interior $C^{1,\al_0}$ and local linearized H\"older
estimates~\cite{GJS}; weighted nonlocal
H\"older/Harnack (Fabes--Kenig--Serapioni analogue)~\cite{BDOR};
weak--viscosity equivalence~\cite{KKL}; higher H\"older
machinery~\cite{BLS}; the $p=2$ template~\cite{PTU}. \\
\hline
\textbf{Conditional} &
The growth estimate $|u(x)|\le C|x-x_0|^{\beta_*}$ at flat branching points
(Thm.~\ref{thm:growth}), under Assumption~\ref{ass:compact}
($C^1_{loc}$-compactness of normalized blow-ups) and
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:L} (degenerate-kernel Liouville); the increment-route
Liouville under \textnormal{(ND)}$+(\star)$
(Thm.~\ref{thm:liouville-incr}); the local limit $s\nearrow1$
(Thm.~\ref{thm:local}). \\
\hline
\textbf{Open} &
Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:L} itself: Liouville for the degenerate linearized
kernel --- equivalently, scale-uniform H\"older/Harnack for $L_{0,h}$; the
Muckenhoupt/BDOR admissibility of $a_0^h$ (route (W), \S\ref{sec:open});
the perturbation lemma (uniform $C^{1,\al'}$ for small right-hand side);
annular nondegeneracy across a two-phase branching point; sharpness of
$\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}$. \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Safe statement.} The theorem this paper proves is
Theorem~\ref{thm:growth}: \emph{for $p\ge2$, $p<\frac2{1-s}$,
$0<\gamma<\frac{p-1}{1+sp}$, if $u$ is a bounded weak solution of
\eqref{eq:main} and $x_0$ is a flat branching point in the sup sense, then,
under Assumption~\ref{ass:compact} and Hypothesis~\ref{hyp:L},
$|u(x)|\le C|x-x_0|^{\beta_*}$ near $x_0$.} No unconditional claim is made,
and no pointwise gradient condition at $x_0$ is used.
